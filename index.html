Problem-Solving Framework

How I Think

Every project I've worked on — from filing a patent to flagging a Fortune 500 security flaw — follows the same underlying structure consultants call structured problem-solving.

01

Diagnose

What is actually broken — and why does it stay broken? I look for the structural failure, not the surface symptom.

Example India's packaged water industry loses billions to counterfeiting. Root cause: packaging design allows refilling with zero friction. The symptom was brand damage. The structural failure was mechanical.
02

Structure

What are the 2–3 root causes? What is the decision tree? I map the problem before touching a solution.

Example Netflix verification "fails open" on network drop. Rather than patching immediately, I mapped the full state machine — isolating the exact handshake being bypassed before writing a single line of code.
03

Recommend & Build

What is the minimum intervention that fixes the core issue? I avoid over-engineering. I prototype, validate, iterate.

Example One-way valve + locking cap for TheNectar. Not a redesign of the entire bottle — just the single mechanical point where the counterfeiting chain could be broken.
04

Communicate Impact

Did it work? What changed? Who needs to know? Every recommendation is only as good as the stakeholder communication around it.

Example Structured a formal vulnerability report to Netflix security via HackerOne — not just the bug, but the root cause analysis, the reproduction steps, and the recommended fix. They acknowledged it as technically valid.
